# General Pet Health Guide

## Warning Signs That Require Immediate Veterinary Attention

The following symptoms in any pet are emergencies — contact a vet or emergency clinic immediately.

- Difficulty breathing, gasping, or blue-tinged gums
- Collapse, loss of consciousness, or seizures
- Suspected poisoning (tremors, excessive drooling, vomiting repeatedly)
- Pale, white, or blue gums (sign of shock or severe anemia)
- Distended or bloated abdomen, especially in dogs (can indicate bloat/GDV)
- Inability to urinate or straining with no output (especially in male cats and rabbits — urinary blockage is life-threatening)
- Severe bleeding that doesn't stop within 5 minutes
- GI stasis in rabbits — not eating, no droppings for 8+ hours
- Eye injuries or sudden loss of vision

## Signs of Illness to Monitor Closely

Contact your vet within 24–48 hours if you notice:

- **Loss of appetite**: Skipping 1 meal may be normal, but 24 hours without eating warrants a vet call (for rabbits, 8 hours is the threshold).
- **Lethargy**: Much lower energy than usual, reluctance to move or play, sleeping far more than normal.
- **Vomiting or diarrhea**: Occasional vomiting is common in cats (hairballs), but repeated vomiting or bloody diarrhea needs attention.
- **Limping or reluctance to put weight on a limb**.
- **Excessive scratching, licking, or chewing at skin**: May indicate allergies, parasites, or infection.
- **Changes in water intake**: Drinking much more or much less than usual can signal kidney disease, diabetes, or other issues.
- **Changes in urination**: More frequent trips, accidents in the house, straining, blood in urine.
- **Discharge from eyes, nose, or ears**.
- **Unexplained weight loss or gain over a short period**.
- **Lumps or bumps** that appear suddenly or change in size.

## Preventive Health Practices

- Keep vaccinations current according to your vet's schedule.
- Apply flea, tick, and parasite prevention year-round.
- Weigh your pet monthly — unexpected changes of more than 5–10% of body weight are worth investigating.
- Schedule annual (or semi-annual for seniors) wellness exams even when the pet seems healthy.
- Keep emergency vet contact information and pet insurance information easily accessible.

## Mental Health and Stress

- Pets experience stress and anxiety which can manifest as physical symptoms or behavioral changes.
- Common stressors: new pets or people in the home, moving, loud noises (fireworks, thunder), changes in routine.
- Signs of stress: hiding, aggression, over-grooming, reduced appetite, excessive vocalization.
- Provide safe, quiet spaces where pets can retreat. Never force interaction when a pet is hiding or stressed.
- Pheromone diffusers (Feliway for cats, Adaptil for dogs) can help reduce environmental anxiety.

## Common Parasites

- **Fleas**: Check for "flea dirt" (tiny black specks in the coat). Treat the pet AND the environment.
- **Ticks**: Check after outdoor walks, especially around the head, ears, and between toes. Remove promptly with tweezers close to the skin.
- **Ear mites**: Dark, crumbly discharge in ears; pet scratches at ears frequently. Needs veterinary treatment.
- **Intestinal worms**: Visible segments in stool, pot-bellied appearance in puppies/kittens, weight loss despite eating normally. Requires deworming medication from a vet.

## Dental Health

- Dental disease affects over 80% of pets over age 3 and can lead to organ damage if bacteria enter the bloodstream.
- Brush teeth regularly with pet-safe toothpaste (never human toothpaste — xylitol is toxic).
- Look for: bad breath, red or bleeding gums, difficulty chewing, loose teeth, pawing at the mouth.
- Professional dental cleanings under anesthesia are recommended every 1–3 years depending on the pet.

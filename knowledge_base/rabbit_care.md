# Rabbit Care Guide

## Diet and Nutrition

Rabbits have a specialized digestive system that requires a very specific diet to stay healthy.

- **Hay is the most important food** — it should make up 80–85% of a rabbit's diet (unlimited supply at all times). Timothy hay is ideal for adults; alfalfa hay only for young rabbits under 7 months.
- **Fresh leafy greens**: 1–2 cups per day per 2 kg of body weight. Safe options include romaine lettuce, cilantro, parsley, basil, dill, and arugula. Avoid iceberg lettuce (too watery).
- **Pellets**: A small supplement — about ¼ cup per 2 kg per day of plain timothy-based pellets. Not the primary food.
- **Fresh water**: Must be available at all times. Use a heavy ceramic bowl or water bottle.
- **Treats**: Fruits (apple, blueberries, strawberries) in small amounts only — no more than 1–2 tablespoons per day. Too much sugar is harmful.
- **Toxic foods**: Onions, garlic, avocado, chocolate, rhubarb, iceberg lettuce, and potatoes are dangerous for rabbits.

## Exercise and Space

- Rabbits need at least 3–4 hours of out-of-cage free roaming time every day.
- A play area of at least 24 square feet is recommended for indoor rabbits.
- Rabbits love to run, jump, and explore — without this, they can develop depression and physical health problems.
- Provide cardboard boxes, tunnels, and platforms for enrichment and jumping.
- Bunny-proof any room they roam in: cover electrical cords, remove toxic plants, block small gaps.

## Handling and Socialization

- Rabbits are social animals and do best with a bonded companion rabbit.
- Handle rabbits carefully — they can injure themselves if they kick and fall. Always support their hindquarters.
- Never pick a rabbit up by its ears.
- Rabbits communicate through body language: thumping = alarm, binkying (jumping and twisting) = happy, flopping = relaxed and content.

## Grooming

- **Short-haired rabbits**: Brush once a week; daily during molting (shedding season) to prevent hairballs.
- **Long-haired rabbits** (Angora): Brush daily to prevent painful mats.
- Rabbits cannot vomit, so ingesting too much fur can cause dangerous blockages — regular brushing is essential.
- Trim nails every 4–6 weeks.
- Scent glands near the genitals may need cleaning every few months.

## Housing

- Minimum enclosure: 4x the rabbit's full-body length in each direction.
- Bedding: Use paper-based bedding or a washable mat; avoid cedar and pine shavings (toxic).
- Spot-clean the enclosure daily; full clean weekly.
- Rabbits are sensitive to heat — keep temperature below 27°C (80°F); they are prone to heat stroke.

## Veterinary Care

- Find a rabbit-savvy exotic vet (not all general vets treat rabbits).
- Annual wellness checkups.
- Spay/neuter is strongly recommended — reduces cancer risk significantly, especially in females.
- Watch for GI stasis (not eating or producing droppings) — this is a veterinary emergency.
